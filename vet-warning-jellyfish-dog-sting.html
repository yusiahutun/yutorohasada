Vets Issue Jellyfish Warning After Puppies Become Unwell From Nasty Sting

 Posted on March 27, 2024  |  6 minutes  |  1231 words  |  Larita Shotwell

Dog owners are being warned about the dangers of jellyfish after two puppies in Sunderland became violently ill from licking one on the sandy shore.

With many popular coastal spots rife with jellyfish following the recent spell of warm weather, owners are being asked to stay extra vigilant — especially as jellyfish have the ability to sting even when they have been dead for weeks.

The owner of the puppies told The Mirror: "Both dogs were projectile vomiting and couldn't breath, constantly licking ground or air. The drive to the vets was horrific. Both dogs were sick everywhere about 10 times each. They are now on oxygen and having medication.

"Please be careful, there were plenty dogs down the beach. I dread the thought of this happening to any other dog."

In response to the incident, Sean McCormack, head vet at tails.com, is urging owners to look out for jellyfish at the beach. In dogs, jellyfish stings may occur on the nose, mouth, paws and areas with little fur to protect them.

"Jellyfish washed up on the beach may not look too dangerous, but the slimy sea creatures can result in a trip to the vets for your four-legged friend," Sean says. "Their stings can be extremely painful to dogs and can result in drowsiness, vomiting and can cause them to struggle to breathe."

What are the signs your dog has been stung by a jellyfish?

Some of the signs your dog has been stung include:

  • Fatigue
  • Swelling
  • Excessive drooling and itching
  • Difficulty breathing
  • Licking the stung area
  • Muscle cramps
  • Crying and stinging
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Sudden change in behaviour

If you notice that your dog has been stung by a jellyfish, make sure you remove any stingers. To do this, soak the area stung in seawater to prevent any further toxins from being released by the tentacles. Never use cold freshwater as this promotes the release of neurotoxins, resulting in more pain.

Justin Aikin

Sean adds: "If you have ice available, apply for 10 to 15 minutes to reduce pain in the affected area, but don't apply the ice or cool pack directly to the skin, wrap it in a towel or bag.

"Avoid letting your pup lick or scratch the affected area, as this can cause the sting to spread. When cleaning the area, make sure your towel and hands are clean, and never rub the area with sand or other rough materials. Next, let the skin and fur dry naturally whilst you ring the vets who will advise you if any further action is needed."

Always consult your local vet if you are unsure what to do. Jellyfish stings can potentially be serious if dogs suffer an anaphylactic reaction, so it's important to act fast.
