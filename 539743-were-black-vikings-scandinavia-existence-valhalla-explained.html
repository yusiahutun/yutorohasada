Who were the Vikings?

Did you know that the Vikings were not a race? According to History, from around A.D. 800 to the 11th century, numerous Scandinavians left their homes to seek fortune elsewhere. These people came from the regions now known as Denmark, Norway, and Sweden, although there are some historical records of Finnish, Estonian, and Saami Vikings.

These people are the ones who are collectively referred to as Vikings or Norsemen (Northmen). Their explorations outside their homeland led them to different territories, more so coastal sites where they raided undefended monasteries in the British Isles.

So, what made the Vikings noticeable and unique? During that time, they attacked different places along the coastline; they were considered uncivilised, and the fact they came from a foreign land made them even harder to understand by the locals. Also, they were not Christian.

Over the years, the Vikings would leave their mark as pirates, raiders, traders and settlers in Britain and Europe. They also made a mark in modern-day Russia, Iceland, Greenland and Newfoundland sections.

Although the primary reason for exploring outside their home has not been discovered, the early ones sought riches. The Viking predecessor pirates preyed on merchant ships in the Baltic Sea and utilised this knowledge to expand their wealth-seeking ventures into the North Sea and beyond.

Were there black Vikings?

Were there ever any black Vikings? Historical records show they were light-haired and fair-skinned, but some Vikings had dark hair and complexion.

Popular culture depicts Vikings as having blue eyes, blonde hair, horn helmets and more. However, this is more about entertainment than historical accuracy.

For centuries, dark-skinned individuals travelled to Scandinavia or were taken there as enslaved people. Therefore, it is automatic that they assimilated into the population over the years through farming, marriage, combat and other cultural factors.

In his book, A History of the Vikings, Gwyn Jones provides evidence that there were individuals with different racial profiles among the Scandinavian people. He states that the ideology that the Vikings were a completely white race of people is factually false, and there were minority races who had brown skin tones as well.

For instance, Harald Fairhair, Norway’s first king’s father, was Halfdan the Black (svarti), and two of his sons were also Halfdan, one named the White (hviti) and the other, interestingly, the Black. Historians like Jones believe that “Black” refers to hair colour.

What does DNA research say about black Vikings?

A team of researchers from different parts of the world analysed bones recovered from more than 80 sites for individuals buried between 2400 B.C. and 1600 A.D. The results of the DNA research were published in an article in the Nature Science Journal.

And it is evident from the findings that the Vikings were racially diverse. Even though the results didn't exclusively point out the existence of a black race, they showed that some people had both Sami and European heritage. It is also important to note that the research analysed a small sample of the massive Viking population.

Who was the first black Viking?

Unfortunately, there is no clear evidence of black Vikings, but their possibility cannot be ruled out either, as evidence has shown that the group were racially diverse. Therefore, it is hard to pinpoint who the first black Viking was.

Who are the famous black Vikings?

There isn’t strong historical evidence that leads to a famous black Viking. However, there have been stories of Vikings who hail from Africa. For instance, there is a 10th-century Viking called “Blacman” that manuscripts depict as having a dark complexion.

Still, it is unclear whether his “dark complexion” was because of race or was merely from getting tanned from spending too much time in the sun.

There is also the story of legendary Norse explorer Leif Erikson, rumoured to have had an African mother.

Did Vikings reach Africa?

There is no doubt that the Vikings explored distant lands in pursuit of wealth and colonisation, but did they reach Africa? One of their areas of exploration that is often overlooked is their exploration of the African coastline. Unfortunately, their history of exploration of Africa remains forgotten.
