Turn your leftover panettone into a tasty trifle

 Posted on March 22, 2024  |  8 minutes  |  1504 words  |  Larita Shotwell

Long a staple of the festive season in Italy, panettone, a sweet, golden bread studded with candied fruit and wrapped up like a present, is fast becoming a fixture of British Christmas too.

The Milanese treat is so popular that some supermarkets say it’s now outselling our classic sticky, stodgy dessert, the Christmas pudding. Sales of panettone have increased by 59 per cent year-on-year at Waitrose.

As a (very proud) half-Italian, I am pleased my fellow Brits are embracing this tradition. One of the best parts of Christmas as a child at my grandparents’ house was being presented with an eye-poppingly large wedge of panettone for breakfast — a tradition my mother and I dutifully keep today, with the grown-up addition of a glass of prosecco for good measure.

Still, I couldn’t eat a whole one and, inevitably, a good third remains untouched each year. Celebrity chefs in the UK have come up with a host of recipes to use up your leftovers in style, from trifle to crumble and scrumptious breakfast fare. But do they pass the Italian taste test?

Giulia Crouch gives verdict on a host of recipes celebrity chefs in the UK have come up with to use up your leftover panettone in style. Pictured: Giulia with the panettone treats

NIGELLA’S NAUGHTY SCRUMPTIOUS SIZZLER

Nigella’s panettone French toast

In the in-between days of Christmas and New Year, exciting dishes using up all the leftovers are called for. This is one of them. It would make the most brilliant brunch or breakfast and can easily be adapted to your tastes. Plus, it’s super simple and is on the table in minutes.

First, I cut two doorstep slices of panettone because I’m very greedy. Then, in a shallow dish big enough to fit your slices of bread, whisk two eggs, 60ml milk and mascarpone. Dunk each slice in the eggy mixture, letting it soak for a minute on each side. Meanwhile, melt a knob of butter with oil in a frying pan.

Now for the fun part. Sizzle those slices in the hot pan, letting them become golden and glorious on each side.

As well as looking so inviting, the panettone French toast will make your kitchen smell fantastic.

Giulia said Nigella’s panettone French toast (pictured) is scrumptious and would go really nicely with crispy bacon and maple syrup

You can serve them with a dusting of icing sugar and a handful of jewel-like pomegranate seeds, as Nigella does, or go wild.

I’m nervous to put this in writing but I think this would go really nicely with crispy bacon and maple syrup, American-style.

Panettone works so well here. It’s crispy but with a squidgy soft interior and cooks a little quicker than a normal piece of bread. Absolutely scrumptious.9/10

BOOZE-FREE TIRAMISU

Gennaro Contaldo’s panettone tiramisu

Giulia said Gennaro Contaldo’s panettone tiramisu (pictured) tastes nice but is nothing special as it's just panettone with cream plonked on top

As a coffee-fanatic, tiramisu is one of my favourite desserts. What’s not to love? Layers of espresso and liquor-soaked sponge with sweet mascarpone cream. Heaven!

Here, Italian chef Gennaro is substituting the normal sponge fingers for slices of panettone but, to my dismay, does not include any alcohol.

As tiramisu recipes go, it’s straightforward. I soak cut-out circles of panettone in coffee and put them in the bottom of individual dessert dishes.

Then beat two egg yolks with 50g sugar before incorporating 250g mascarpone. Top the panettone with the cream and dust with powdered coffee.

Hmm, seems a little uninspiring but I reserve judgment for the tasting. It is, as I expected, mediocre. Part of the joy of tiramisu is the different layers — but this is just panettone with cream plonked on top.

It still tastes nice but it’s nothing special. As for the use of panettone instead of sponge fingers, I’m not convinced. I don’t think the dried fruit in the panettone belongs in the dish and it’s a little weird combined with the coffee flavour.

I also think tiramisu should always incorporate booze — you can use rum or marsala or, for the ultimate coffee experience, pick Kahlua, a coffee liqueur, like my mum does. 4/10

CHOCOHOLIC’S PERFECT PEAR-ING

Thomasina Miers’s pear, panettone and choc trifle

Giulia said Thomasina Miers’s pear, panettone and choc trifle (pictured) has a nice contrast between mellow, cold cream and sharper, tangy fruits

I was very excited about this recipe. Poached pears topped with booze-soaked panettone, mascarpone custard and chunks of dark chocolate — it’s an undeniably good idea and easier to make than you might expect.

I start by peeling, coring and chopping six pears. It’s tedious and slippery but, once my pears are under control, I start to poach them in a mixture of the juice of an orange, 150g sugar and a thumb-sized piece of grated fresh ginger. To make the custard, you beat together four egg yolks and 100g sugar before folding in a pleasingly generous 500g of mascarpone.

Then, to create a moussey effect, whisk three egg whites into stiff peaks and fold them into the custard with a lashing of marsala wine. The result is nothing but dreamy. I spoon a lot of it into my mouth before realising I need it for the trifle. At this point it’s really just an assembly job. In go the pears, poaching liquid, thickly-cut slices of panettone, marsala wine and 50g dark chocolate chunks, followed by that wonderful mascarpone cream.

Top with flaked almonds and more dark chocolate and set in the fridge for a minimum of three hours, though I left mine overnight.The next day I dug in with a big spoon, and I was disappointed. The nice thing about a trifle is the contrast between mellow, cold cream and sharper, tangy fruits, but with this there was too much sweetness and nothing to balance it.

If I were to make it again I’d add a lot more booze and less orange. The panettone, however, works perfectly as a constituent and would in any variation of trifle. It’s an ideal soaker-upper of flavour while still retaining its shape. 6/10

BLINGY BREAD AND BUTTER PUD

Nigel Slater’s marmalade panettone pudding

Giulia said Nigel Slater’s marmalade panettone pudding (pictured) is the ultimate comfort food and the best way to transform leftover panettone 

Traditional bread and butter pudding is good — but I promise you, this is better. Instead of using white sliced, which you then have to sprinkle with sultanas, use panettone and cut out a step.

Nigel Slater’s recipe also incorporates marmalade, which adds a little touch of bitterness that works perfectly against the cooked custard.

Simply slice 325g of panettone and spread marmalade on each bit. Whisk four eggs with 500ml milk and three drops of vanilla extract. Layer half your slices of panettone in a buttered, deep, ovenproof dish and pour over half your mixture. Repeat the process until everything’s used up and put in a 180c (355f) oven for 35 minutes.

It smells divine and, with eye-catching golden flecks from the marmalade, looks beautiful too. Serve it warm with a drizzle of double cream and settle into the most comforting bowl of goodness.I love the contrast of the squidgy, soft, eggy bits against the crispy, golden bits on top. This is ultimate comfort food and, in my opinion, the best way to transform leftover panettone. 10/10

CROWD-PLEASING CUSTARD CRUMBLE

Panettone crumble, from online recipe website delicious.com.au, inspired by Italian food specialist Eataly’s panettone with custard

Giulia said Panettone crumble (pictured) can be whipped up in no time and is a definite crowd-pleaser - especially for little ones 

Panettone and custard seems to be the trendiest dessert of the moment — in London restaurants at least. The City-based food hall and restaurant Eataly has more than 40 different kinds of panettone, and knows its Italian baked goods inside out.

I admit to going a little off piste with this recipe, but I reckon it only makes it better.

Instead of pulsing the panettone in a food processor to make a fine crumb and toasting it in the oven for 20 minutes, I chopped it up into bigger chunks and put it in an air fryer (you could use a toaster or the grill).

Once crisp, I rained down the golden nuggets of panettone over a big bowl of 800g lemon zest-infused custard. Then, to take the crumble idea even further, I added a couple of handfuls of mixed berries to the top and sprinkled over a few flaked almonds.

Yum, yum, yum. The crispy bread against the soft and creamy custard, spiked with the acidity of the berries, is a killer combo. Blitzing the panettone into a very fine crumb would have meant you lost it completely.

You can whip it up in no time and it’s a definite crowd-pleaser — especially for little ones.8/10
