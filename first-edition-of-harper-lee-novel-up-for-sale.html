First edition of Harper Lee novel up for sale

 Posted on July 12, 2024  |  2 minutes  |  320 words  |  Aldo Pusey

All images courtesy of Peter Harrington

A first edition of To Kill a Mockingbird (1960) – the Pulitzer Prize-winning novel by Harper Lee, whose death at the age of 89 was announced today – is on sale at Peter Harrington, the UK's largest rare-book seller.

The edition, priced at £25,000 and featuring the original bright unrestored jacket, is one of a catalogue of 50 extremely rare books and manuscripts launched by the specialist book seller. 

Courtesy of Peter Harrington

Titles on sale range from classic to contemporary literature and span history, drama and children's literature, among other genres. There are two especially valuable items, each priced at £275,000, in the catalogue. The first is a six-volume, first-edition set of The Holy Land & Egypt and Nubia by David Roberts (1842–1849; pictured above), which comes in a deluxe format with 246 lithographed plates that have been coloured by hand. The other is a first-edition copy of F Scott Fitzgerald's The Great Gatsby (1925), which includes a handwritten inscription from the author addressed to Harold Goldman, a screenwriter at MGM whom Fitzgerald calls "the original 'Gatsby' of this story".

Courtesy of Peter Harrington

Another highlight of the collection is a copy of the fourth folio of Shakespeare's comedies, histories and tragedies that dates from 1685 and is priced at £125,000. With April marking the 400th anniversary of the Bard's death, the book would make a very special gift for a collector this year.

Other top finds include a first edition of Kenneth Grahame's The Wind in the Willows(1908), priced at £125,000; and two original 1931 typescripts by Winston Churchill, priced from £30,000.

To accompany the launch of the catalogue, Peter Harrington has produced a series of videos in which some of its expert team share information about the 50 rare books and manuscripts. Watch the videos here, browse the catalogue here or visit the store at 43 Dover Street, London W1.
