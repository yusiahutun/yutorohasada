Is Szane a Luxury Brand? Szane's Designer Explains for Its 10-Year Anniversary

 Posted on January 1, 0001  |  6 minutes  |  1229 words  |  Martina Birk

French direct to consumer fashion label Sézane has an origin story that reads like a fashion fairytale for the internet age. Shoppers who already own the label's cuddly button-up cardigans in bulk or who've seen it worn by Katie Holmes, Ashley Graham, and Selena Gomez may already know the tale by heart—but it bears repeating.

The brand that would eventually become a certified B.Corp with stores on two continents and modest ambitions to reach women around the world began as a humble eBay storefront. Morgane Sézalory, Sézane's founder and a true vintage aficionado, would source decades-old luxury clothing and add her own slight modifications: a new button here, an extra embellishment there. Each week's upload would sell out in minutes.

The process fused Sézalory's lifelong appreciation for luxury fashion with an education in cutting, sewing, and design—all that would inspire her jump to create a standalone brand. "When you look at some pieces from the forties or fifties and they are still here today and look so good [...] it's like your teaching of what you want for your own collection," Sézalory told me.

Ten years later, propelled by vintage-inspired designs and word-of-mouth growth, Sézane is bigger than ever but far from a "peak" or over-saturation.

Sézane is profitable, a rarity for online-first fashion businesses. It's worn with regularity by A-list actors and royalty, including Princess Kate, and capitalizing on its celebrity approval with a Los Angeles office and permanent store. The interiors in Sézane's spaces are so often complimented (and photographed) that they’re now available to shop, with the debut of Les Compsantes, Sézane's homeware and interiors line. A collaboration of floral dresses and patchwork quilted coats with Sea New York promptly sold out in March. On Sunday, May 28, Sézane will likely repeat that history with the release of a capsule co-designed with FARM Rio, featuring matching sets, swimsuits, and dresses in a range of botanical prints.

The eBay storefront is gone, but the spirit lives on.

Sézane trades in a version of "French Girl style" that speaks to Americans, evident in lines that still form outside its New York City store on collection release days, or the rate at which online-only pieces sell out (quickly). The fifteen items making up a list of "Top Parisian pieces" on its site are unpretentious wardrobe staples: a Breton stripe sweater, a khaki trench coat, a leather flap bag with minimal hardware, and so on. It's just as popular in its hometown—the last few times I visited Sézane's Paris flagship known as L'Appartement in 2018 and 2022, crowds have appeared outside.

"Maybe what is French, [...] is what always we say is 'effortless,'" Sézalory said of her brand's close ties with Paris and ideas about its style. By 'effortless,' she means items that feel pulled together with minimal styling and a whisper of makeup.

There's a contradiction in this look: "It looks effortless but actually there is so much effort," she said. Over the course of our conversation, she used "quality" 23 times to describe her focus as a leader. Sézalory is concerned with quality and supply chain more so than fitting a made-up fashion ideal. It's because she hopes the label's careful attention to sourcing, factories, and those quintessentially French designs will endure. More than becoming simply popular and admired like other brands, "I want every piece to be able to go through generations," she said.

Sézane

Morgane Sézalory, Sézane’s founder, says she doesn’t like to define the brand’s aesthetic. "What I love is just to always remind myself how many personalities every woman can actually have," she says.

Sézane is Morgane Sézalory and her taste, down to the just-so placement of a floral vase its New York City store or the precise way a belt hits on a wrap dress. Even the name: It's a combination of her first and last.

Her office, a palatially large space with huge, arced windows in Sézane's Paris headquarters, is artfully cluttered with thick coffee table books and antique prints. But save the occasional interview with a journalist or a brand event, Sézalory is not the sort of founder who necessarily wants to be the out-and-about face of Sézane. She likens her collections to paintings, obsessing over the palette and the feelings they evoke. As the artist, she's content to stay behind the scenes, even during the brand's milestone year.

"I'm just so like a girl next door to people, but at the same time, I think they can tell how much heart and passion, how well I want to do for every consumer," she said.

It's both the nature of the brand and Sézalory's quiet but fervent passion that drew in collaborators for its tenth anniversary. Katia Barros, co-founder of FARM Rio, met Sézalory in Paris after her label hosted a pop-up at the famed department store Le Bon Marche. She had been a fan herself for years, and noticed a connection between their two home cities of Rio de Janeiro and Paris.

Noam Galai//Getty ImagesSézane’s profile has also grown in the U.S. with celebrity outfit placements, like the Sézane coat Selena Gomez wore to an event last fall.

"We share the same joie de vivre and have something that is hard to be put in words—they call it je ne sais quoi, and we call it borogodo," Barros explained. "When Sezane came to us about working together, we knew that this was an incredible opportunity to bring two brands and cities to live in blending equal parts unpretentious elegance and tropical freshness."

Max Mumby/Indigo//Getty Images

Kate Middleton has worn Sézane on several occasions. Recently, she finished an outfit for a royal appearance with a pair of Sézane earrings.

What Sézalory attempts to thread through each collection is "the magic of everyday life," or pieces that bring a sense of something special to an otherwise quotidian piece, whether it’s a collaboration or seasonal capsule. It's clothing that captures the excitement of tracking down vintage without the hunt; another manifestation of the joy she sees in her two daughters running through their Left Bank home, trying on her designs with abandon.

The founder doesn't think this spark has to appear in a prototypical "French girl" look. "In some countries but also in France, you have some women who come for only essential pieces. Some women come from like more creative [pieces] and some women come just because they want the perfect white shirts and they know they will find what they love. I think every woman feels free to take whatever [speaks to her]," she said.

The scale has ballooned far beyond at-home eBay alterations to a global business, but "I look at every day if it was the first day," Sézalory said. As Sézane moves on through its anniversary year and on to the next, the founder says she'll never see her career-long passion project as having "arrived." That way, she never loses the magic.
